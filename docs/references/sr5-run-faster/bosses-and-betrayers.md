# Bosses and Betrayers -- Johnsons and Fixers

**Source:** Run Faster, p.196–200
**PDF Pages:** 198–202

---

## Overview

This section defines the roles of Mr. Johnson and Fixers in the Shadowrun ecosystem, along with the three-phase lifecycle of a typical shadowrun engagement: The Meet, The Run, and The Handoff. It provides practical guidance on etiquette, negotiation tactics, threat assessment, and payment collection from both the runner and Johnson perspectives.

---

## Rules

### Mr. Johnson -- Definition and Role

- **Mr. Johnson** is anyone who hires a shadowrunner for a job. The term is generic -- any employer is "your Mr. Johnson."
- In the vast majority of cases, the work is illegal or at least in a grey area.
- By hiring runners for illegal activity, Mr. Johnson becomes a criminal and exposes himself to risk. This shapes Johnson behavior: they are personally invested, self-protective, and often stereotypical in their caution.
- Johnsons' reactions are influenced by their background and cultural norms.

### Fixers -- Definition and Role

- **A fixer** is a middleman who provides goods and services to both sides of the shadowrun engagement (Johnsons and shadowrunners).
- Primary function: build a stable of reliable shadowrunners and connect them with Johnsons.
- Fixers also provide ancillary services: connecting runners with discreet mechanics, street docs, arms dealers, and other specialists. They may also buy and sell illicit goods directly.
- The key attribute of a good fixer is **reliability** -- building trust with both sides.

### Fixer Payment Models

Two primary compensation models for fixers:

1. **Full Payment Model**: Mr. Johnson pays the entire sum to the fixer, and the fixer pays whatever he can get away with to the shadowrunners. The fixer becomes the de facto Mr. Johnson.
   - Advantage: Fixer can make more money.
   - Disadvantage: Fixer's payment is tied to mission completion, increasing risk and incentivizing micro-management.

2. **Fee-for-Service Model**: The fixer charges Mr. Johnson a fee for providing a crew, then steps out.
   - Advantage: Fixer gets paid quickly and moves on to the next deal.
   - Disadvantage: Less total money.
   - **Most fixers prefer this model.**

3. **Finder's Fee**: A fixer may charge a finder's fee to both sides (to the supplier and to the runner). This is common and negotiable.

4. **Favor Trading**: If cash-strapped, a fixer may trade for favors instead of liquid nuyen.

### Lifecycle of an Engagement

The engagement has three phases:

#### Phase 1: The Meet

The initial meeting with Mr. Johnson. Key guidelines:

**Presentation:**

- Project confidence, not bravado.
- Demonstrate expertise by asking expert questions.
- Show backbone -- don't be a pushover.
- Dress appropriately for the meeting location (not for the Johnson).
- Exception: If hired for high-society infiltration, showing high-society appearance helps.

**Gear Etiquette:**

- Holster all weapons visibly (pistols in holsters, SMGs in slings, rifles slung).
- Maximum outerwear: armored jacket. No bigger.
- You don't need to conceal weapons from Mr. J -- just show you made an effort to holster them.
- Prepare for potential ambushes, especially with a new Johnson.

**Magic Etiquette:**

- **Never use magic during meetings.** Most Johnsons are well-versed in what magic can do and are wary of mood/mind-controlling spells.
- If Mr. Johnson notices magic, you almost certainly won't get hired.
- Successfully using mind-control on a Johnson who later realizes it earns a serious black mark.

**Information Gathering:**
Three things to determine at The Meet:

1. As many details about the job as possible.
2. Does Mr. Johnson intend to pay you, and how much?
3. Does Mr. Johnson intend to kill you?

**Questioning Protocol:**

- Ask all questions about the mission now -- this is the time.
- Do NOT ask about the Johnson, his employer, or motives. Figure that out yourself.
- Focus on operational details: job specifics, guard count, reinforcements, arrival timing, security measures.
- The Johnson may not know everything. Milk him for what he does know.

> **Ambiguity:** The section mentions some Johnsons come from cultures where asking questions earns disdain. No specific mechanics are provided for cultural modifiers -- this is purely narrative guidance for GMs.

**Warning Signs -- Non-Payment:**

- The money offered is too good, or the Johnson doesn't negotiate hard.
- Mr. Johnson shows up with overwhelming bodyguards.
- Mr. Johnson acts aggressively during the meeting.
- The team has no established reputation.

**Warning Signs -- Liquidation Intent:**

- Johnson has a history of killing teams (check background/past jobs).
- Johnsons who kill teams earn black marks from savvy teams quickly.
- Check the Johnson's rep in the shadows.

**Rapport de Force:**

- The only fairness in the shadows is what you create for yourself.
- If you can't create unpleasant consequences for non-payment, the Johnson has no incentive to pay.
- Never hand over goods until you have been paid.

#### Phase 2: The Run

The operational phase after The Meet.

**Communication Rules:**

- Minimize contact with Mr. Johnson during the run. The default is "don't call me."
- Every communication risks exposure for both sides.
- As the mission progresses, increase discretion.
- Legwork (Matrix, magical, physical recon) is expected -- just be discreet.

**Surveillance Awareness:**

- Targets may detect they are being watched and begin counter-investigation.
- Shadowrunners are hard to find (they don't officially exist).
- Johnsons are typically known in shadow player circles -- easier to trace.
- Contacting your Johnson may lead surveillance teams to set up on the Johnson, which then leads back to you.
- **Unwritten rule:** Johnsons don't attack other Johnsons. Shadowrunners, however, are fair game.

**When to Call Mr. Johnson:**

- Only call if the mission is fundamentally compromised.
- Solving problems without calling is a mark of maturity and professionalism.
- Good reason to call: mission parameters changed drastically (e.g., target already extracted by another team, target became a bug spirit host).
- CFD complications may also warrant a call.

**Leverage and Payment During the Run:**

- After mission completion but before handoff, the only incentive for the Johnson to pay is reputation and avoiding runner retaliation.
- If the job involves killing a target, get paid almost entirely before pulling the trigger.
- Getting 80% paid before the final action is reasonable; getting only 20% upfront with 80% deferred gives the Johnson incentive to not pay.

> **Cross-reference:** Street reputation mechanics are referenced but not detailed here. See SR5 CRB for Notoriety and Street Cred rules.

#### Phase 3: The Handoff

The final exchange -- returning to meet Mr. Johnson with the completed objective.

**Two Core Rules:**

1. **Never ask for more than was agreed.** This is a sure way to earn a bad reputation and ensure you never work again.
2. **Control the environment.** Tell Mr. Johnson where to meet. Set up an ambush position. Get your sniper in place overlooking the meet. Have your hacker scan the Johnson's team for weapons detection. Let Mr. Johnson see you are in charge here -- visible weapons, bead on his people. The goal is intimidation to ensure payment.

**Leverage Dynamics:**

- Everything leads to this moment. You must have leverage.
- Avoid runs where you do something and then have nothing to show (sabotage, assassination). In those cases, you already did the work with no physical deliverable to withhold.
- Extractions, thefts, and similar runs where you hold the objective are best for leverage.
- Johnson reputation is a factor: Johnsons who don't pay get bad rep. Killing any team (even green runners) is taboo.

## Implementation Notes

- This section is primarily GM guidance and narrative rules, not mechanical crunch. It does not introduce dice pools, thresholds, or new game mechanics.
- For a digital tool, the most implementable elements are:
  - **Engagement phase tracker**: Track which phase a run is in (Meet / Run / Handoff) as part of campaign management.
  - **Johnson warning signs checklist**: Present GMs with a checklist of warning signs during The Meet phase.
  - **Contact role classification**: Use the Johnson/Fixer distinction to tag NPC contacts in the contact system.
  - **Fixer payment model**: Track how a fixer NPC is compensated (full payment vs fee-for-service) as a contact attribute.
- The cultural Johnson backgrounds mentioned on p.198 are expanded later in the chapter (not in this section) -- flag for future extraction.
