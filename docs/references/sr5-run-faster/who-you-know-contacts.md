# Who You Know -- Contact Rules

**Source:** Run Faster, p.172-180
**PDF Pages:** 174-182

---

## Overview

This section expands on the core contact rules (SR5, p.385-389) with detailed breakdowns of contact service types, payment mechanics, a chip/favor debt system, group/organization contacts, relationship qualities (Blackmail, Family), maintenance rules, and a quick contact personality generator. Contacts provide six types of services, accept four forms of payment, and relationships are tracked via a "chip" system representing mutual debts and favors.

---

## Rules

### Breakdown of Contact Types

Contacts may perform multiple services, but each contact has one primary specialty. Using a contact for a secondary purpose is considered a favor (p.389, SR5). Six types of services/support exist:

1. **Legwork** -- Chasing down the data trail and answering questions that may not be found on the Matrix (p.387, SR5).
2. **Networking** -- Putting the right people in the same room, getting them to talk, and launching mutually beneficial relationships (p.388, SR5).
3. **Swag** -- Acquiring items the character wants, or buying/fencing goods the character picked up on the job (p.388, SR5).
4. **Shadow Services** -- A special kind of favor (p.388, SR5). Contacts who perform shadow services have a job to do and use their skills on the character's behalf for a form of payment besides the usual cash.
5. **Personal Favors** -- A special kind of favor (p.388, SR5). These contacts have power, influence, or authority. The character wants them to bend rules, turn a blind eye, or whisper suggestions.
6. **Support** -- Contacts who may not have power, a job to exploit, or money to lend, but have the character's back. May have a talent, a cool toy, or a place to crash. Often friends and minor contacts not central to the shadowrunner's business.

> **Cross-reference:** Each service type references the corresponding SR5 Core Rulebook page. Legwork = p.387, Networking = p.388, Swag = p.388, Shadow Services = p.388, Personal Favors = p.388.

### The Cost: What Does a Contact Want?

Every contact has a preferred transaction type. If their price is not met, the character owes the contact (see I Owe You One, p.176). If the price is not paid in a proper time frame, the contact notices, and there will be consequences: the contact's Loyalty can go down, the character's reputation might take a hit, or worse (see Nothing Personal, Just Business, p.177).

#### Cash

Digital nuyen is the standard currency for contacts. A service fee is added to the price of the item or service depending on the relationship (Loyalty) and the effort to find the item (tied to Availability Rating). Not all contacts want nuyen. Alternatives:

- **Corporate Scrip** -- Can only be spent at corporate-owned stores. Corporate-based contacts may prefer it as it leads to fewer questions.
- **Hard Currency** -- Physical bills and coins. Less traceable than credstick but easier to counterfeit.
- **Foreign Electronic Currency** -- ECC, Euro, peso, UCAS dollar via moneychangers (who take their fee in nuyen).

#### Services

Contacts often accept services in lieu of cash. Three categories:

- **Shadow Work** -- A job using the character's shadowrunning skills (sabotage, data theft, retrieval, bodyguarding, etc.). Most common form.
- **Contact-Related Work** -- Using the character's skills in the course of the contact's normal job. Free skilled labor. Examples: street doc asks character to courier medical supplies; talismonger sends character to collect reagents; law enforcement contact asks character to canvass a neighborhood.
- **Drek Jobs** -- Off-the-wall tasks unrelated to the contact's or character's profession. The contact trusts the character enough to get it done. Examples: exterminating pests, chaperoning, samba lessons.

#### Barter

Some contacts prefer trading goods to minimize electronic footprint. Trades can be:

- **Directly related** to the contact's work (e.g., specific guns for a gun dealer in exchange for repair services).
- **Wide-ranging** (e.g., information for goods, rare items for services).
- **Ambiguous value** -- Items whose worth changes depending on the contact's skill and opinions. Works best when precise values are not critical.

For contacts who already seem to have everything, they may be turned by unique, magical, or rare items -- manuscripts, black market rarities, etc. These can form the basis of side trips or new adventures.

#### Other

A back door to payment: learn about the contact personally. Are they married? Kids? Hobbies? Vices? This can help find the right item or offer to unlock their services. Risk: playing off a contact's vice may make them less effective. If an addiction is supplied, the GM should check on the contact's addiction (p.414, SR5) to see if they further succumb.

### Cutting a Deal: Optional Qualities for Barter Goods

Bartering involves guesswork about perceived value. The GM can add these optional qualities to help quantify worth:

- **Provenance** -- Recorded evidence tying the item to a famous person or event. Double the value for items tied to recent events and living people. Triple the value for more historical events and deceased people.
- **Vintage** -- For every decade of the item's age, add one percent to its value, up to a maximum of ten percent (for century-old items).
- **Master Crafted** -- Better materials, inspected for quality, possibly hand-assembled. Add twenty percent to the value of the item.

### I Owe You One (Gaining Chips)

When a character cannot pay a contact in cash, the character may promise "I owe you one." This creates a debt tracked through a system of "chips."

**Gaining chips (contact gains chips on character):**

- If the character successfully requests a favor from a contact (p.389, SR5), the contact gains chips with the character equal to the Rating of the favor.

**Repaying chips:**

- When repaying, the contact should generally demand twice the value of the favor in payment. A Rating 2 favor requires two Rating 2 favors in return, or one Rating 4 favor.
- An agreed-upon cash payment, based on approximately twice the market value of the original favor, can also be accepted.
- Payment with valuable goods can substitute for cash if the contact is amenable.

**Contact cashes in chips:**

- A contact can cash in a chip or several chips to gain a favor from the character, possibly including pro-bono side missions that drag other characters in.
- If the character decides not to honor the favor, the relationship degrades, possibly reducing the Loyalty of the contact (especially if the initial favor was Rating 3 or higher).

**Character earns chips on contact:**

- Sometimes the character does good work for a contact but the contact has nothing to give (or the character does not want anything). Or the contact requests a favor and the character completes it. This is a chip for the character. These chips have a relatively long shelf life and can be collected from any contact.

### Improve Relationship

The GM is free to roleplay how the character can improve a relationship with a contact. Chips that the character and contact may owe each other can be a factor. The character can spend chips and time in weeks to improve the relationship up one level. The cost is equal to the loyalty level the character is trying to achieve.

| Current Rating     | Target Rating      | Chip Cost | Time (Weeks) |
| ------------------ | ------------------ | --------- | ------------ |
| Just Biz (1)       | Regular (2)        | 2 chips   | 2 weeks      |
| Regular (2)        | Familiar (3)       | 3 chips   | 3 weeks      |
| Familiar (3)       | Got Your Back (4)  | 4 chips   | 4 weeks      |
| Got Your Back (4)  | Buddy For Life (5) | 5 chips   | 5 weeks      |
| Buddy For Life (5) | Buddy For Life (6) | 6 chips   | 6 weeks      |

> **Ambiguity:** The text says "the cost is equal to the loyalty level the character is trying to achieve" and gives the example of Rating 1 to Rating 2 costing "two chips and two weeks." It is unclear whether advancing from Rating 5 to Rating 6 costs 6 chips and 6 weeks, or if there is a cap. The Loyalty Rating names (Just Biz through Buddy For Life) are listed at Rating 1 through 5 in SR5 p.389, but the text here references Rating 6 as still "Buddy For Life."

> **Cross-reference:** Loyalty Ratings: Just Biz (1), Regular (2), Familiar (3), Got Your Back (4), Buddy For Life (5-6). See SR5 p.389 for favor rules.

If the characters complete work that brings significant benefits to the contact, the GM may improve both the Connection rating and the Loyalty of the contact at the end of the adventure.

### Future Service

A character who is low on funds can spend chips from any contact to pay for services. The GM determines how many chips are needed.

### Favors

A character's chips can be used to get favors from a contact. Favors (p.389, SR5) can be service other than those the contact normally offers, but the action must be within the contact's abilities as decided by the GM. Each chip spent adds one die to the Negotiation Test to gain the favor, up to a maximum bonus of +4. If successful, the contact gains chips equal to the Rating of the favor, minus any remaining chips the character has with the contact. If the character has a lot of outstanding chips, the favor may only partially reduce their debt.

### Group or Organization Contact Options

A character might have a contact that is an entire organization rather than a single individual.

**Key differences from individual contacts:**

- The organization is faceless -- cannot do favors, cannot gain or owe chips.
- Loyalty is limited to 1.
- The character has some contractual obligation to the organization (see strictures, p.129, Street Grimoire).
- Some organizations require the character to have a SIN so they can be clearly identified.
- Group and organizational contacts are limited to **legwork or networking services** only.
- The contact provides bonus dice to tests made during legwork or efforts to find help.
- The contact represents the character using nameless/faceless channels within the organization. Downside: it leaves a data trail, and select people in the organization know what the character is up to.

**Selecting an organizational contact:**

- Treat the Karma spent at character creation for an organizational contact as part of the expenditure for positive qualities, making it part of the limit that can be spent on qualities.

### I Know a Guy

With the GM's permission, "I Know a Guy" allows the player to use Edge to pull a contact from the character's past. Requirements:

- Players must spend Edge points equal to **twice** the desired Connection Rating of the contact.
- The resulting contact's Loyalty starts at 1 (though the GM may adjust).
- The Edge spent on this contact does not refresh until the next mission/adventure.
- After the mission, the character can spend the normal amount of Karma to add the new contact permanently. If Karma is not spent, the individual fades back until Edge is used again.

### Social Networking

Some tasks may be too big for one contact. If the GM permits, a character can get contacts together through the commlink, sharing face time and information instantaneously. The character makes a Leadership + Charisma [Social] Test, with hits being the limit on the number of contacts a character can manage for this task. The Contacts then can make a teamwork test (p.49, SR5) for completing the task. The cost of social networking is that the character has to pay each contact individually for this service, not just their portion of it; the price is multiplied by the number of contacts involved.

### Nothing Personal, Just Business

The contact is loyal to the character (according to starting Loyalty rating), but for how long? Players should consider the nature of the relationship from the contact's perspective -- are they an old army buddy, a blackmail victim, a romantic interest? This affects how the contact behaves.

### A Test of Loyalty

If a contact is under duress, their loyalty to the character may be tested. Make a Loyalty Test: roll the character's Intuition + the contact's Loyalty rating, and apply the hits as bonus dice to the usual resistance roll. Other actions done or not done by the character can also lead to Loyalty Tests.

### Paying Off Debt

Chips can be paid off through the contact's preferred method of payment within an acceptable timeframe (see Repayment Table). If a payment is not made within the timeframe, the contact makes a Loyalty Test with a threshold equal to the number of weeks that have passed.

- **Success:** Loyalty endures, contact gives the character a break and another week.
- **Failure:** Loyalty is reduced by 1. This test occurs every week until the debt has been paid.
- If the contact turns down a character's request for a favor that would pay off some debt, a Loyalty Test is made with a threshold of the number of chips that would have cost. This test is made regardless of whether the player wanted to pay off the debt in a different way.

### Nature of the Relationship

The Loyalty rating defines most of the relationship, but additional qualities can refine it.

#### Blackmail

The character has leverage over the contact. Loyalty becomes a measure of how much leverage exists. Key rules:

- Loyalty Tests still apply as normal, but it is about having the blackmail exposed, not giving up the character.
- With blackmail, a contact's Loyalty (Leverage) **does not diminish**, nor can a contact leave.
- The character **must use intimidation** to get this contact to do anything.
- Favors with a blackmailed contact **do not cost the character**.
- If the contact fails a Loyalty (Leverage) Test, they no longer care about being blackmailed. The contact is then **no longer a contact** (see Burning Bridges). The character gains a point of Notoriety, and there are now people out for payback (use Connection Rating to determine their Professional Rating).
- A blackmailed contact's relationship **cannot be fixed**.
- Contacts with this quality cost an additional **2 Karma**.

#### Family

Blood is thicker than water. Key rules:

- Treat contacts with the Family quality as having **Loyalty one higher** than actual when performing Loyalty Tests.
- The character earns **1 less chip** than normal when asking favors.
- Chips spent improving relationships also cost **one less**.
- On the downside, contacts are **less diligent** in getting things done for family. Reduce Loyalty by 1 in performing their job (p.387, SR5). This includes extra dice, discounts, etc.
- Buying a contact with this quality costs an additional **1 Karma**.

### Maintaining Contact Relations

Contacts are maintained through usual interaction. If the player does not keep up some sort of interaction between character and contact (once every [Loyalty] months), the GM can make a Loyalty (2) Test to see if the contact has lost interest or given up on the character.

- **Failure:** The contact's Loyalty Rating drops by 1.
- If the contact's Loyalty drops to 0, the contact may be lost altogether (see Burning Bridges, p.179).

### Using Intimidation

Intimidation can be used with negotiations through a Teamwork Test (p.49, SR5) in order to gain a favor or service without paying for it or falling into chip debt. Costs:

- Immediate reduction of Loyalty by 1.
- Removal of the player's ability to improve the relationship between the contact and character.
- Every use of intimidation continues to reduce Loyalty by 1 until the character burns the bridge.
- Exception: contacts who are being blackmailed (Loyalty reduction does not apply to them from intimidation).

### Using Con/Seduction

During downtime, an opposed Con (p.141, SR5) Test can be used to maintain the Loyalty of a contact.

- **Success (character does not get more net hits than current Loyalty):** Loyalty remains the same even if the contact had to make a Loyalty Test that week.
- **Character gets more net hits than current Loyalty:** Contact raises Loyalty by 1.
- **Failure:** Contact's Loyalty reduced by 1.
- This test can be made only once in a downtime period per contact.
- If the character fails to pay off debt in the appropriate amount of time or does not redeem a favor asked by the contact, any Loyalty Test that fails reduces their Loyalty by **2**.
- The character also earns a point of **Notoriety** for their deception.

### When Your Reputation Precedes You

Characters with enough Street Cred or Notoriety can be known by other runners, contacts, law enforcement, and organized criminals.

- When recognized, **Street Cred** becomes a bonus to the character's Social limit (p.368, SR5).
- If the character has **more Notoriety than Street Cred**, it becomes a hindrance in forming new contact relations.
- For each **2 points of Notoriety over Street Cred**, improving Loyalty costs additional **1 Karma or 2 chips**.

### Burning Bridges

Contacts whose Loyalty Ratings are reduced to 0 are getting ready to wash their hands of the character. If the player does not invest any time/interest in that contact to fix the relationship, that contact is **lost** to that character by the start of the next job/adventure. All chips owed by or debited to the contact are erased at this point. The contact becomes part of the character's history (see I Know a Guy, p.177). A blackmailed contact's relationship **cannot be fixed**.

### Do I Know You? (Recognition Test)

This test determines if characters know someone they encounter during a job, or if they themselves are recognized. It is a **Memory Test** (p.152, SR5) with a threshold from the table below. The highest of either Notoriety or Street Cred of the subject plus public awareness reduces this threshold. The higher awareness value also determines if the subject is known in a positive or negative context.

> **Ambiguity:** The text says "Only Public Awareness reduces threshold for the general public." This implies Street Cred and Notoriety do NOT reduce the threshold for general public recognition -- only Public Awareness does. For runners and crime/law enforcement, all three (Street Cred, Notoriety, Public Awareness) reduce the threshold.

### Quick Contact Personality Generator

GMs can quickly flesh out a contact's personality, service fee, and stats. Contacts can be built as Prime Runners (p.385, SR5) or use the Contact Quickstats sidebar, rolling D6 for each item. The GM may further change or enhance the contact.

#### Contact Quickstats

**Stats:** Distribute 18 points among the attributes, starting with the minimum for the contact's metatype and adding from there.

**Skills:** Each point of Connection Rating changes the amount of points for attributes, skills, and cash. See the Contact Quickstats table in JSON for full breakdown.

## Implementation Notes

- **Chip tracking** is central to this system. Each contact needs a `chips` integer field (positive = character owes contact, negative = contact owes character). This is per-character, per-contact.
- **Relationship qualities** (Blackmail, Family) should be modeled as optional flags on a contact-character relationship, each with specific Karma cost modifiers and behavioral overrides.
- **Organization contacts** are a distinct contact subtype with hardcoded constraints: `loyalty: 1`, `serviceTypes: ["legwork", "networking"]`, `canDoFavors: false`, `canOweChips: false`.
- **Improve Relationship** is a downtime action: validate chip cost = target loyalty level, time = target loyalty level in weeks.
- **Favor dice bonus** from chips: clamp at +4 maximum.
- **Maintaining contacts** requires tracking last interaction date per contact and comparing against `loyalty` months.
- **Reputation effects** need Street Cred and Notoriety values from the character to compute Loyalty improvement cost penalties.
- **I Know a Guy** is an Edge spend action during play; track the temporary contact separately until Karma is spent post-mission to make it permanent.
- **Payment preference** on each contact affects the debt/Loyalty Test flow -- if the contact's preferred payment type is not met, the repayment timeframe table kicks in.
- **Loyalty Test on debt** uses threshold = weeks passed (for overdue payment) or threshold = chip count (for refused favor payoff).
